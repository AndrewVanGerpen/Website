About Me

My decision to study aerospace engineering came later than most incoming college students selecting their area of study. As I transitioned between high school and college, I knew I wanted to pursue a career involving the application of math and physics, but I was not particularly passionate toward any specific field. I sifted through the numerous major titles within the college of science & engineering at the University of Minnesota, looking at class titles and their respective synopses. I was particularly drawn to the upper level aerospace classes. Specifically, Orbital Mechanics, Aerodynamics, and Instrumentation.​

Since starting as a naive freshman in 2017, my desire to discover and innovate, particularly in the areas of flight electronics and software, has grown greatly. I have worked hard to supplement my coursework with real application; to make sense of the seemingly abstract concepts that compose aerospace mechanics. I am eager to step beyond the research and projects at the University level and into the aerospace industry. I hope to find myself surrounded by people who are as passionate about innovation as I am, and to work on a team dedicated to solving the pressing engineering problems we are faced with today.​

Alka-Rocket Challenge

The alka-rocket challenge is a rocketry competition organized by Bayer with the limitation that the propulsion substance must only consist of Alka-Seltzer and water. I have been a member of the small group from the university’s rocket team that has competed in the finals of this competition since 2017. This was my first extracurricular project in college, and I often reflect on how important the experience was.

I credit the alka-rocket challenge with sparking my interest in avionics. As a freshman, watching senior members of the team design, program, and assemble an operating altimeter from scratch was fascinating, and that experience helped me become a critical member in the following years.

The alka-rocket challenge also helped me become familiar with many tools available at the University to aid in rocket fabrication. Among these tools are laser cutters and 3D printers. For the 2017 competition the nosecone and body tube were fabricated from solid-filament 3D printers, while the fins were lasercut from thin sheet fiberglass. Since then, we have worked away from the solid-filament prints and replaced those components with a combination of liquid prints and custom composite pieces.

To maximize the apogee of our launch vehicle, we decided during year one that it would be smart to leave the propellant on the ground and shoot the vehicle out like a potato cannon.

Each year, we have iterated the rocket’s parameters using OpenRocket, a rocket simulation software, to help build a stable yet aerodynamic design.

Aerobiology Payload

The payload that we coined as Aerobiology is a microbe collecting payload for sampling between 60,000 and 80,000 feet altitude. The payload uses a combination of GPS and pressure data to decide an appropriate time to sample. Ensuring appropriate sample window is crucial in this case because the filters collecting microbes must remain sterile until reaching sampling altitude.

The sampling process includes a series of pumps and valves. The valves open and pumps begin sucking air through tubes that are coupled with filters. The tubes are extended several feet beyond the outside of the payload on a protruding carbon arm. The opening of each tube is at the tip of the carbon rod, several feet from the payload, to mitigate the potential of collecting particles disturbed by the balloon on ascent.

A teensy 3.5 microcontroller operates the payload, and sensors/breakouts include a DALLAS temperature sensor, honeywell pressure sensor, xBee S2 radio, and a ublox GPS module. The pumps and valves operate using a 12V battery and are toggled using a 5V latching relay.

Tripolli Level 1 Certification

During the summer of 2019, I spent many of my evenings working on a rocket that I would later fly to receive my Tripoli Level 1 certification. I designed the rocket using a OpenRocket, an open source software where users specify the materials, shape, motor, and various parameters of a rocket to determine rocket stability and apogee. The rocket body was made from 1/4” thickness 3" diameter cardboard tube, the fins from 1/8” thickness G10 fiberglass, and the avionics bay from 1/4" MDF. The nosecone was made from PLA 3D print filament and designed using SolidWorks.

​

In the launch pictured below, the rocket was recovered using a single deploy parachute at apogee. A stratologger altimeter recorded the flight and deployed the parachute. While the rocket has only been flown for my level 1 certification flight, it could be launched for level 2 certification with dual deploy parachutes.

VERTICAL balloooning system

Summer of 2020, during the COVID-19 pandemic, I decided to take on the ambitious task of designing an easy to use yet customizable flight system with logging and telemetry of various sensor data such as IMU data, temperature, pressure, and GPS data. I used a teensy 3.5 microcontroller and wrote the software in Arduino (C++ with built-in functions to aid in interfacing with sensors).

I began a simpler version of this project in the spring of 2020. The original goal was to create a board that housed a few standard flight sensors on a board with a small footprint to act as a parasitic data logger. By the beginning of summer, I decided to add greater functionality with the addition of a complex radio relay system and robust ground station software for in-flight communication and tracking.

For most of the 2020 summer I was writing and iterating both the flight and ground software for VERTICAL. At that time I had only assembled one board for testing as I didn’t anticipate having the opportunity to do flight testing. After receiving clearance to resume balloon launches in July, I quickly populated three additional PCBs and spent a week testing and iterating the radio relay system. Two of the boards were used to collect data for a group of graduate students from the university’s visual arts department. Since this group was particularly interested in GPS and temperature data, no sensors beyond the stock VERTICAL sensors were added.

The data logging and intra-stack communication among all four devices in the first flight proved the reliability of the VERTICAL boards. I hope VERTICAL will continue to provide all researchers and students an easy way to obtain and analyze atmospheric data.

APRS Parsing Python GUI

The APRS parser is a Python GUI that saves, parses, and graphs APRS data. One of our primary forms of high-altitude balloon tracking is the Stratotrack APRS transmitter. This device transmits data at ___ MHz, the standard frequency for the APRS network. The data is then picked up by HAM radio operators who brodcast the data to the internet. If our data is broadcast, we can view it on aprs.fi.

These APRS transmitters provide us with great supplementary data to be used in post-flight analysis. However, The cryptic format in which it is recorded online makes it difficult to locate the relevant data within the packet.Parsing every packet for relevant values by hand would take hours. This is why I decided it would be worthwhile to write a program that parses each packet, taking advantage of certain “delimiters”, to pluck relevant values such as time of packet transmission, GPS coordinates, altitude, and temperature.

After parsing, the data is recorded in a CSV file so it can be neatly viewed and manipulated in Excel. The GUI also provides a list of variables to plot on either axis of a 2D plot and allows the user to save these plots as JPG images. The plots are automatically marked with axis labels and a title.
